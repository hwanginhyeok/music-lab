# I'll Call You Spring — English Adaptation v1

> Original: "봄이라고 부를게" (Korean, acoustic ballad-pop)
> Adapted for: Cool & smooth contemporary pop (synth, bass hook, falsetto, syncopated groove)
> Core wordplay preserved: "call" = to name / to phone | "spring" = season / to return
> Tone: Cool, restrained, smooth — emotions leak through details, not declarations

## Adaptation Notes

### Wordplay Architecture

| Original (Korean) | English Adaptation | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| 봄 = spring (season) | "spring" = season | Direct |
| 봄 = 보다 (to see/miss) | "wanna see you" — paired with "spring" | "call you spring" + "wanna see you" creates the dual meaning through juxtaposition |
| 부르다 = to name | "I'll call you spring" = I'll name you spring | Direct |
| 부르다 = to call (phone) | "I just called you" = I phoned you | Direct |
| 투르르 투르르 = phone ringing | "ring... ring..." | Natural English onomatopoeia |
| 같은 말인데 이상해 | "same words... funny how they've changed" | The phrase "I'll call you spring" means both naming and phoning — same words, shifted meaning |

### Section-by-Section Choices

**Verse 1**: The original uses cherry blossoms ("벚꽃 같다고 하면 뻔한 거잖아" = "saying you're like cherry blossoms would be too obvious"). In English, I kept the self-aware meta-commentary but dropped the cherry blossom reference, using "something blooming I can't shake off" instead — less culturally loaded, more universal. The coffee steam image ("커피잔 위에 김") translates perfectly and stays.

**Pre-Chorus**: "계절을 빌려 너를 부를게" (I'll borrow a season to call you) becomes "I'll borrow a season instead" — clean setup for the chorus hook.

**Chorus 1**: The Korean original directly names the season as the person ("너를 봄이라고 부를게" = I'll call you spring). This works identically in English. "네 탓" (your fault) becomes "feeling like your fault" — the casual blame-shifting that masks real longing. "I'll blame you for the butterflies" is more natural in English than "I'll say the excitement is your fault."

**Verse 2**: "택시 두 대가 반대로 꺾어진 골목" (two taxis turning opposite ways at a corner) — this hyper-specific breakup image is the Kim Eana signature. "Two cabs splitting at the same dead end" preserves the visual while adapting to English idiom ("dead end" adds a layer of meaning about the relationship).

**Chorus 2**: The emotional wall breaks. Korean original: "센치해져서가 아니라 / 네가 보고 싶어서 전화했어" (it's not sentimentality / I called because I miss you). English version maintains the structure of denial-then-truth: "It's not that I got sentimental / I just wanted to hear your voice."

**Bridge**: "투르르 투르르" becomes "ring... ring..." — the universal phone sound. The rehearsed weather excuse ("요즘 날씨 낭만 있지 않아?") becomes "nice weather lately, right?" — the same deflection, culturally adapted.

**Vocalisation**: Added a falsetto "ooh" section that doesn't exist in the Korean original. This fits the new style (The Weeknd / Frank Ocean territory) and gives a breath before the emotional final chorus.

**Final Chorus**: "봄은 매년 오잖아, 근데 너는" (spring comes every year, but you—) is the gut punch of the whole song. The dash, the unfinished sentence. "Spring comes back every year — but you don't" completes just enough to hurt, while the em-dash preserves the weight of the original's trailing silence.

### Phonetic Design for New Style

| Section | Sound Design | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Verse 1 | Soft consonants (m, n, l, w), open vowels | Groove-friendly, laid-back delivery |
| Chorus | Short syllables, repetitive hook ("I'll call you spring") | Sing-along requirement, bass hook sync |
| Verse 2 | Harder consonants allowed (t, k in "truth", "cabs", "splitting") | Emotional weight, contrast with verse 1 |
| Bridge | Fragmented phrases, pauses | Simulates phone call tension |
| Final Chorus | Mix of smooth and broken phrasing | Emotional release + vulnerability |

### Syllable Count Comparison

The new style (syncopated groove, bass hook) demands shorter, punchier lines than the Korean original.

| Section | Korean (avg syllables/line) | English (avg syllables/line) | Note |
|---|---|---|---|
| Verse | 10-12 | 8-10 | English naturally shorter for groove |
| Chorus | 9-11 | 7-9 | Sing-along needs space |
| Bridge | 5-8 | 4-6 | Sparse, conversational |

### Cultural Adaptations

1. **Cherry blossoms** → Generic "blooming" — cherry blossoms in English carry Japanese cultural weight more than Korean; "blooming" is more neutral
2. **코끝이 빨개진** (red nose tip) → "noses turning red" — works in both cultures
3. **날씨 핑계** (weather excuse) — universal behavior, translates directly
4. **혼자만 봄** (spring alone) → "spring for one feels strange" — preserves the loneliness-in-brightness paradox
5. **Overall tone shift**: Korean original is warm-melancholic acoustic. English version is cool-melancholic R&B/pop. The emotional restraint is the same, but expressed through different cultural registers — Korean warmth vs. Western cool detachment.
